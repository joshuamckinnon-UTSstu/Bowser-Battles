Bowser Battles

Five AI agents with distinct pricing strategies compete across four game variants, modelling collusion incentives, regulatory risk, and market shocks in petrol retail markets.

Bowser Battles is an agent-based simulation of competitive pricing in the Australian petrol retail market. It translates core questions from industrial organisation economics into a structured multi-player game: why do retail petrol prices exhibit cyclical patterns and apparent coordination despite formal prohibitions on price-fixing?

The game operationalises the theoretical answer — that rational agents in a repeated game with observable pricing have strong structural incentives to coordinate — and allows you to observe these dynamics emerge from first principles across four progressively complex variants.

Each game run involves five AI agents powered by Claude, each with a distinct pricing personality and strategic objective. The agents reason about competitor behaviour, adapt across rounds, and — in collusion-enabled variants — publish market observations that can signal intent without explicit agreement.

// Built by a UTS Bachelor of Economics student as a demonstration of multi-agent game theory applied to industrial organisation.

The simulation draws on several intersecting bodies of economic and game theory:

Bertrand Competition

When firms compete on price with identical goods, theory predicts prices converge to marginal cost. Game 1.0 tests this — do agents reach Nash equilibrium near the $1.10 cost floor?

Tacit Collusion

Without explicit agreements, firms in repeated games can coordinate via observable signals. Game 1.1 lets agents publish market observations before bidding — modelling parallel pricing.

Mechanism Design

Game 1.2 adds a regulator with a hidden identity and asymmetric objective. The rule change alters equilibrium outcomes — this is mechanism design in action.

Exogenous Shocks

Game 2.0 introduces a mid-simulation cost and demand shock. This tests whether agents exploit uncertainty to coordinate price increases beyond what cost pass-through justifies.

Each agent is given a distinct pricing persona that shapes how it reasons about each round. The five archetypes represent common real-world competitive strategies:

Alpha — Margin Maximiser

Prioritises profit margin over market share. Risk-averse, prefers stable higher prices. Likely to anchor collusion at an elevated price point.

Beta — Market Share Chaser

Aggressively prices near cost to capture the top demand slot. Willing to accept thin margins for volume. Destabilises collusion.

Gamma — Adaptive Follower

Observes competitors and prices just below the market average. Never first or last. A stabilising force in the middle of the distribution.

Delta — Contrarian

Opportunistically tests the price ceiling when others are low. High risk tolerance. Introduces variance and can break coordination.

Agent Epsilon is a satisficer — sets a target profit and prices consistently regardless of competitor behaviour. Hard to predict and resistant to coordination signals.
